The AI Stack: Who's Really Building the Future

February 2026 · 8 min read

The artificial intelligence boom has a habit of putting a few famous names in the headlines — OpenAI, Nvidia, Google — while obscuring the far more interesting reality underneath. AI isn't built by a handful of companies. It's built by an entire civilization of interdependent layers, each one impossible without the one below it. Miss any single layer and the whole thing collapses.

Here's how it actually works.

The AI Stack — three interdependent layers that make modern AI possible.

Layer 1: The Hyperscalers — The Companies You've Heard Of

These are the firms building and deploying the models that the world interacts with. They sit at the top of the stack, but don't mistake visibility for independence — every single one of them depends entirely on the layers beneath.

OpenAI

OpenAI is the company that made AI a household conversation. GPT-4 and ChatGPT turned a niche research field into a global phenomenon overnight. They move fast, ship aggressively, and have a complicated but deeply important partnership with Microsoft, which has invested over $13 billion and baked OpenAI's models into everything from Word to Azure.

Anthropic

Anthropic is OpenAI's most credible rival — and in many ways its philosophical opposite. Founded by former OpenAI researchers, Anthropic is obsessed with AI safety. Their model, Claude, is widely considered the most thoughtful and nuanced of the frontier models. They're not just trying to build powerful AI — they're trying to figure out how to build AI that doesn't go wrong as it gets more powerful.

Google DeepMind

Google DeepMind is the sleeping giant that everyone keeps underestimating. Google invented much of the foundational research that made modern AI possible — the Transformer architecture that powers every major LLM today came out of Google in 2017. Their Gemini models are formidable, and they have one asset nobody else has: the most valuable data distribution network on earth. Every search, every YouTube video, every Gmail.

Meta AI

Meta AI took a different bet from everyone else — open source. While OpenAI and Anthropic keep their models closed, Meta released their Llama models freely to the world. The bet is that a thriving open ecosystem benefits Meta more than a walled garden would. It's a fascinating strategic gamble, and so far it's working — Llama has become the foundation for thousands of AI projects worldwide.

xAI

xAI is Elon Musk's entry into the race, built around the Grok model and deeply integrated with X (formerly Twitter). The unique angle is data — X gives xAI access to a real-time stream of human conversation that no other lab has. Whether that translates into a genuinely superior model remains to be seen, but the asset is real.

Amazon

Amazon isn't building frontier models in the same way — their play is infrastructure. AWS is where a huge portion of the world's AI workloads actually run, and their investment in Anthropic gives them a seat at the frontier table without having to win the model race themselves.

Layer 2: The Hardware Architects — The Companies Making It Possible

You cannot run a large language model on a laptop. Training GPT-4 required tens of thousands of specialized chips running for months. The companies designing those chips are, arguably, more important to the AI revolution than the model builders themselves.

Nvidia

Nvidia is the most important company in AI that most people couldn't have named five years ago. Their GPUs — originally designed for video games — turned out to be perfectly suited for the matrix mathematics that powers neural networks. Their latest flagship, the RTX Pro 6000 Blackwell, represents the cutting edge of what a single GPU can do — built on the Blackwell architecture, it delivers massive leaps in AI inference performance and is designed for professionals and data centers that need serious compute power in a single card. But Nvidia's real moat isn't just the hardware — it's CUDA, their software platform that developers have been building on for over a decade. Switching away from Nvidia doesn't just mean buying a new chip, it means rewriting your entire software stack. That's a moat most competitors haven't been able to cross.

AMD

AMD is Nvidia's most serious challenger, and the competition is heating up. Their Instinct MI350 series is their current flagship AI accelerator, purpose-built for data center workloads and designed to go head-to-head with Nvidia on raw performance at a more competitive price. AMD's strategy is straightforward — offer comparable performance, better availability, and avoid the supply bottlenecks that have frustrated Nvidia customers. The gap is closing. Major cloud providers are increasingly offering AMD-powered instances, and companies tired of paying Nvidia's premium are paying attention. AMD won't dethrone Nvidia in the near term, but they don't need to — even capturing 20% of the AI chip market would be a massive business.

Broadcom

Broadcom is less visible but critically important. They design custom AI chips — called ASICs — for Google, Meta, and others who want hardware tailored to their specific models rather than general-purpose GPUs. When Google says their TPUs are more efficient than Nvidia chips for their workloads, Broadcom is a big part of why.

ARM Holdings

ARM Holdings doesn't make chips — they design the underlying architecture that most chips are built on. Every iPhone, most Android phones, and an increasing number of data center chips run on ARM's blueprints. As AI moves to edge devices (running models on your phone rather than in the cloud), ARM's position becomes even more central.

Layer 3: The Foundry — The Invisible Foundation

This is the layer that almost nobody talks about, and it might be the most important of all. The companies here don't build AI products. They build the physical infrastructure that makes every chip on earth possible.

TSMC

TSMC — Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company — is one of the most geopolitically significant companies in human history. They manufacture the chips designed by Nvidia, AMD, Apple, and almost everyone else. The most advanced chips in the world, built at 3 and 4 nanometer processes, can only be made at TSMC's fabs in Taiwan. This single point of concentration is why the Taiwan Strait has become one of the most watched pieces of ocean on earth.

ASML Holdings

ASML Holdings sits one level deeper still. TSMC can only make advanced chips because of machines built by ASML — a Dutch company that holds a complete global monopoly on Extreme Ultraviolet lithography machines. These machines, which cost $150–$200 million each and take years to build, are the only way to print circuits small enough for modern chips. There is no substitute. No other company on earth can build them. ASML is, quietly, one of the most irreplaceable companies in existence.

SK Hynix, Micron & Samsung

SK Hynix, Micron, and Samsung supply the memory that makes AI inference possible. High Bandwidth Memory — HBM — is the specialized RAM that sits directly on AI chips and feeds them data fast enough to keep up with their computational power. Without a steady supply of HBM, Nvidia's chips are significantly less useful. SK Hynix currently leads in HBM production, which has made them one of the most important suppliers in the entire AI supply chain.

The Takeaway

When you hear that a company is "investing in AI," it's worth asking which layer they're operating at. A hyperscaler without access to chips is just a research lab. A chip designer without TSMC's fabs has a blueprint and nothing else. And none of it works without ASML's machines humming in a clean room in the Netherlands.

The AI revolution is not a software story. It's a civilizational infrastructure story — one that runs from the most cutting-edge machine learning research all the way down to rare earth minerals and precision optics. Understanding the stack is understanding the real race.
